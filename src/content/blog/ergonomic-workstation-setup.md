You bought the perfect chair and desk. But if your setup isn't configured right, you're still risking injury.

This guide will help you set up your workstation ergonomically, step by step.

## The Golden Rules

Before we dive in, memorize these five rules:

1. **Eyes level with top of monitor**
2. **Elbows at 90° when typing**
3. **Feet flat on floor (or footrest)**
4. **Wrists straight, not bent**
5. **20-26" from eyes to screen**

Get these right, and you'll avoid the back pain, neck strain, and wrist problems that plague remote workers.

---

## Step 1: Chair Height

Start with your chair. This is the foundation of your entire setup.

### How to Adjust:

1. Sit with feet flat on floor
2. Adjust seat height so thighs are parallel to the floor
3. Ensure 2-3 fingers fit between the seat edge and the back of your knees
4. Adjust armrests so shoulders are relaxed and elbows are at 90°

### Why It Matters:

If your chair is too high, your feet dangle — this puts pressure on the back of your thighs. If it's too low, your knees are above your hips, which puts strain on your lower back.

The sweet spot: thighs parallel to the floor, feet flat on the ground.

---

## Step 2: Desk Height

Your desk should match your chair height — or be adjustable to match.

### How to Set It:

With elbows at 90°:
- If sitting: your desk height should allow elbows to rest at a 90° angle
- If standing: desk at elbow height

### Pro Tips:

- **If your desk is too high** and can't be adjusted: raise your chair and use a footrest
- **If your desk is too low**: you might need to raise the entire desk or get a desk converter

The goal: your shoulders should be relaxed, not shrugged up toward your ears.

---

## Step 3: Monitor Position

This is where most people go wrong. It's also the easiest to fix.

### The Correct Position:

- **Top of monitor at eye level** — You shouldn't have to look down or tilt your head up
- **Monitor 20-26" from eyes** — That's about arm's length
- **Monitor centered in front of you** — Not off to the side
- **Tilted slightly back** — 10-20° tilt helps reduce glare and neck strain

### How to Measure:

1. Sit in your chair at your normal working position
2. Have someone hold a ruler or tape measure horizontally from your eye level down to where the top of your monitor would be
3. Adjust your monitor (or monitor arm) so the top is at that height

### If You Have Multiple Monitors:

- **Primary monitor** — Directly in front of you
- **Secondary monitor** — To the side at a slight angle
- **Both at the same height** — Don't have one higher than the other

This setup prevents you from constantly turning your neck, which causes strain over time.

---

## Step 4: Keyboard and Mouse

Your input devices should be positioned to keep your wrists straight.

### The Setup:

- **Keyboard centered in front of you** — Not off to one side
- **Mouse right next to the keyboard** — No reaching! Reaching for the mouse is a common cause of shoulder and neck tension
- **Wrists straight, not bent up or down** — Consider a keyboard tray if your desk is too high
- **Keyboard at elbow height** — Same rule as desk height

### Pro Tips:

- If you type a lot, consider a **keyboard tray** — it lets you position the keyboard lower than desk height
- Use a **mouse pad with a wrist rest** — but only for your palm, not for typing
- If you use a keyboard tray, make sure there's clearance for your legs

---

## Step 5: Lighting

Good lighting prevents eye strain and makes you more productive.

### The Rules:

- **No glare on your screen** — Position your desk perpendicular to windows, not facing or backing them
- **Task lighting for documents** — A small lamp helps when reading papers
- **Avoid backlighting** — A window behind your monitor creates glare and strains your eyes

### Quick Fixes:

- Close blinds during bright sunlight
- Use a monitor hood or positioned light to reduce glare
- Consider a **monitor light bar** — it lights your keyboard without illuminating the screen

## The Ergonomic Checklist

Run through this before you call it done:

- [ ] Chair height adjusted so thighs are parallel to floor
- [ ] Feet flat on floor (or footrest)
- [ ] Elbows at 90° when typing
- [ ] Top of monitor at eye level
- [ ] Monitor 20-26" away
- [ ] Monitor centered in front of you
- [ ] Keyboard centered
- [ ] Mouse next to keyboard (no reaching)
- [ ] Wrists straight, not bent
- [ ] No screen glare

## Common Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

### Mistake #1: Monitor Too Low
**Problem:** You slouch to see the screen.
**Fix:** Raise your monitor with a stand or monitor arm.

### Mistake #2: Desk Too High
**Problem:** Your shoulders shrug when you type.
**Fix:** Raise your chair and use a footrest, or get a keyboard tray.

### Mistake #3: Monitor Too Far
**Problem:** You lean forward to see the screen.
**Fix:** Move the monitor closer. You should be able to touch the screen with your fingertip at arm's length.

### Mistake #4: Reaching for Mouse
**Problem:** Mouse is too far from keyboard.
**Fix:** Move the mouse closer. Your elbow should be at your side, not extended.

### Mistake #5: No Lumbar Support
**Problem:** Lower back aches after long sessions.
**Fix:** Adjust your chair's lumbar support, or add a lumbar pillow.

## When to Take Breaks

Even with perfect ergonomics, sitting too long is bad for you.

### The 30-60 Rule:

- **Stand up every 30-60 minutes** — Set a timer if you need to
- **Stretch for 2-3 minutes** — Simple neck rolls, shoulder shrugs, and back twists
- **Walk around** — Get blood flowing

### Quick Stretches at Your Desk:

1. **Neck rolls** — Slowly roll your head in a circle, 5 times each direction
2. **Shoulder shrugs** — Raise shoulders to ears, hold 3 seconds, release. Repeat 10 times.
3. **Wrist circles** — rotate wrists in circles, 10 times each direction
4. **Chest opener** — Clasp hands behind back, squeeze shoulder blades together
